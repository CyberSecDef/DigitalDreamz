The mirror in the hallway had stopped reflecting whoever walked past. It now reflected whoever had walked past last week. A delay had set in. No one was concerned. The mirror eventually caught up, or did not. By then it didn't matter.

There was a mirror in the attic that reflected the room from the opposite wall. Anyone standing before it saw the back of their own head. The reflection turned around when the original did not. Eventually the reflection began to gesture at things. No one in the attic could see what the reflection was pointing to, only that it pointed.

A polished spoon held up to the morning light. In the spoon's curve, a small upside-down kitchen. In that kitchen, a small upside-down person, holding a small upside-down spoon. The recursion stopped at the third spoon, where the surface had been slightly worn.

The window behaved as a mirror when it rained. Whoever passed it during a storm saw themselves in the wet glass and also the rain in themselves. Some rain went through the body of the reflection and emerged on the other side, falling into a courtyard that did not exist outdoors.

In the lake, the trees were upside-down. This was unremarkable. What was unusual was that the lake's trees had different leaves than the trees on the shore. Different species, different seasons. The two forests reached toward each other through the lake's surface and never quite touched.

The barber's mirror had been hung crooked for so long that the reflections grew up crooked. Customers leaving the shop walked at a slight tilt for the rest of the day, until they passed another mirror and were corrected, or were not.

Two facing mirrors in a small room. What appeared in each was not the other's reflection but the other's intention. The mirrors had agreed on this between themselves. Anyone standing between them saw two slightly different selves, and the difference between them was the room.

A puddle in the alley that reflected a sky from a different city. The clouds in the puddle moved at a different speed. A bird crossed the puddle's sky without ever appearing in the actual sky overhead.

The bathroom mirror, when fogged, showed the writing of whoever had been in the bathroom previously. The previous person had not written anything. The fog wrote anyway. Words appeared and faded as the steam thinned. No one read fast enough.

There was a tradition in the village that on the longest night of the year, every mirror was turned to face the wall. The villagers said that on this night, mirrors went elsewhere, and what came back the following morning was not always what had been left.

A small mirror in a music box. When the lid opened, the mirror reflected the inside of the lid, which was nothing. When the lid closed, the mirror reflected the room. The music box had been closed for forty years.

The river occasionally reflected a sky that was missing one star. Astronomers had not been consulted. The missing star was different each time. Nothing in the heavens above was actually missing. The river was simply selective.

A child holding a hand mirror to the floor. In the floor-reflection, the ceiling. In the ceiling, a chandelier the room did not have. The child did not mention it. The chandelier in the mirror swayed gently, as if a window had been opened in another room.

Mirrors stacked face-to-face in a closet. Whatever was between them no longer required a viewer. They reflected each other, and in the depth of that mutual reflection, things began to occur that did not require the closet.

The mirror in the entryway had begun to fog when no one stood before it. Sometimes it fogged in the shape of breath. The breath was always slightly too large to belong to whoever lived in the house.

A blind man kept a mirror in his pocket. When asked why, he said the mirror was for others, not for himself. Anyone who borrowed the mirror reported afterward that they had seen something they had not been able to see in any other mirror.

The shop window's reflection was always one customer ahead of the shop. Whoever passed the window saw themselves entering, then a moment later actually entered. The shopkeeper had grown accustomed. He greeted the reflection first and the customer second, and never confused the order.

A teapot polished to a mirror finish. Anyone holding it close saw their own face fragmented into several small surfaces, each of which showed a slightly different age. The youngest was the curve of the spout. The oldest was the bottom, where the kettle had touched the stove for a long time.

The hallway mirror reflected everyone the same height. A child and an adult passing it appeared in it at identical heights. The hallway was unconcerned with this. The mirror was simply democratic.

A pond at the bottom of the garden, in which the reflection of the moon was always full, regardless of the moon's actual phase. The pond was older than the moon, the gardener said. The gardener was not entirely wrong.
