Of the city of Veshtri it was said that the inhabitants forgot one street each generation, and that the forgotten streets continued to be walked by the ones who had forgotten them. The forgotten streets did not disappear. They simply ceased to be remembered. A traveler arriving in Veshtri could not find the inn until the innkeeper appeared, and the innkeeper appeared only because the inn had not yet been entirely forgotten.

In the city of Anlomar, all stairs descended. The architects had decided this in a previous century. Anyone wishing to ascend was obliged to leave the city, walk in a wide circle through the surrounding fields, and return through one of the gates that opened onto the upper terraces. The gates were built into the foundations and reached only by descending. Ascent was a matter of orientation, not direction. The Anlomarans did not consider this a hardship. They considered it the truth.

The city of Brindeu had no map because its streets rearranged themselves at night. Each morning the inhabitants found the city as it now was and proceeded as if it had always been so. The bakery that had stood near the river was now beside the cemetery. The school had moved into a tower that the previous evening had been a granary. The children, having spent the night dreaming of the school's old position, sometimes arrived at the granary and were taught nonetheless, the lessons bending to the building. By the afternoon they had forgotten the school had ever been elsewhere.

Mehrishal was a city of doors. Walls were rare. The city's planners had believed that any partition was a kind of address, and so they built only doors, each one freestanding in fields and squares, each opening onto wherever it opened onto. Mehrishal had no inside and no outside. Travelers passing through often did not realize they had arrived until they had already left, and even then, only because the doors they passed through were no longer the same doors.

In the city of Sarev, time moved at different rates in different districts. Citizens carried small notebooks recording what year it had been when they last visited each quarter. Letters delivered between districts arrived sometimes years before they were written. The post office maintained a back room for letters that had not yet been composed. Children born in the slow districts grew old in the fast ones; the elderly went to the slow districts to wait, and the waiting was longer than any of their lives.

Of Yelashka, only the bells remained. The city had been there. The bells continued. Whoever rang the bells now was not anyone anyone could find. Travelers crossing the empty plain heard the bells and turned back, having received whatever had been sent. The plain itself had memory. The memory was the bells.

The city of Quenestre rebuilt itself every fifty years according to the dreams of its oldest inhabitant. When the oldest inhabitant died, a new oldest inhabitant began dreaming the city, and over the next fifty years the buildings shifted, the streets relearned themselves, the river found a new course. No one in Quenestre regretted the changes. Each new city was the continuation of the dream of the previous one, the dream of a dream of a dream. The first city, if there had been a first city, was no longer remembered.

In Olshen, the dead lived in the upper stories of the houses they had owned. They did not appear, but they kept the lights on. The living slept on the ground floors. At night, light spilled from the upper windows of every house, and the streets were lit by the dead. The dead read books that the living had not yet written. The dead listened to weather that had not yet arrived.

There was a city, name unknown, that existed only in the silence between sentences. Whoever spoke of it ceased to be in it. Whoever wrote of it found the city absent from the page. The city was the unspeakable, and the unwritten, and the unphotographed. The city had a population. The population had never been counted. The counting itself would have ended the city.

The city of Themar was built around a well that no one had ever drawn from. The well was sacred, and the sacredness consisted in the not-drawing. Around the well, a small square. Around the square, a market. Around the market, the city. Around the city, the country. The country drew its water from elsewhere. The well held what it held. Themar prospered, and the well remained full.
