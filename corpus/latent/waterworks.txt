A pond into which had fallen a single key. The key was not retrieved. The pond, over many years, became key-shaped, and the key, sunk in the silt, became pond-shaped, and eventually the two could not be distinguished.

In the deep well behind the village, the water was occasionally something other than water. No one drew from the well at those times. The villagers had learned to recognize, by the angle of the light on the surface, whether the water was water or whether something else had risen.

The mirror over the basin in the unused bathroom had begun to reflect a different bathroom. The other bathroom was similar but not identical. The towel rack was in the wrong place. There was a window where the original had a wall. Whoever stood before the mirror saw both bathrooms at once, and the reflection of the self in each was the same reflection, looking back from two places.

A river that flowed only when remembered. Whoever forgot the river found the riverbed dry. Whoever remembered it found the water there, cold, moving, ordinary in every respect except that it required the remembering to exist.

The threshold of the back door had been wet for as long as anyone could recall. Stepping over it, one felt briefly the wetness of a different place, a coastline perhaps, or a kitchen in another country, where the floor was always damp because the sea was always near. The back door opened onto an ordinary garden, but the threshold remembered the sea, and the sea, somewhere, remembered the threshold.

A glass of water set on a windowsill in a room no one entered. After a time, the glass was always full. Whoever filled it was not seen. Whoever drank from it was not seen either. The glass had become its own custom.

Rain that fell only inside one room of the house. The room had no leak. The ceiling was sound. Yet rain fell, gentle, persistent, across the carpet, and the carpet absorbed the rain as if rain had always been part of the carpet, and the carpet did not rot, and the room did not flood, and the rain continued.

A pool in the courtyard so still that it reflected things that were not there: a tower that did not exist in the city, a moon at noon, a face leaning over the water that no one in the household resembled. The pool reflected what it wished. The household did not look into it often.

The river behind the house had two banks. On the near bank, the household. On the far bank, a household identical to the household, which kept the same hours, ate the same meals, lit the same lamps in the evening. Neither household crossed. Neither acknowledged the other. The river ran between, careful not to choose.

A glass of milk left out overnight became something other than milk. Not spoiled. Not curdled. Simply other. By morning the glass held a substance white and patient that resembled milk in every respect except that it had begun to consider what it might prefer to be. The household poured it down the drain. The drain accepted it without comment.

The bathtub in the upper room held water that had not been poured. The water remained at a constant level. It was warm in winter and cool in summer. No one bathed in it. The bathtub was understood, by those who knew the house, to be doing its own work, and that work did not concern the household.

Steam from a kettle that had not been lit. The steam rose through the kitchen, curled around the lamps, gathered at the ceiling, descended again as the faintest possible mist. The kitchen smelled of hot water. There was no hot water. The kettle was cold to the touch, and patient, and certain.

A drop of water on the windowsill that did not evaporate. The drop had been there for years. Whatever held the drop in place was not a force the household could name. The drop was bright, perfect, unchanged. Visitors sometimes noticed. The household did not explain.

A fountain that ran only at the moment no one was looking. Whoever turned to face the fountain found it dry, the basin empty, the stone of the spout warm but motionless. Whoever turned away heard, behind them, the soft fall of water beginning. The fountain was reliable in its unreliability. The household had learned not to test it.

A mirror in the hallway that occasionally showed the hallway from an earlier century. The hallway was the same. The light was different. A woman walked through it sometimes, in clothing the household had only seen in old portraits, going about her business. She did not notice the household. The household did not interrupt her. The mirror held both centuries, and the mirror was patient.
