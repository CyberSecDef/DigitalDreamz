A clock in the corridor that ran half-time. Visitors checking it found themselves an hour late or an hour early, depending on how long they had been in the building. The clock was correct on the way in and correct on the way out, but never in between. The corridor approved.

There was a calendar in the kitchen that had only six months. The other months had been removed, the housekeeper said, because the family did not need them. They lived only in the six months they kept. The other months passed elsewhere, in other people's homes.

The pocket watch had stopped, but it told the time anyway. Whoever opened it found that the hands moved if they were not looked at directly. The watch was honest, in its way. It simply preferred privacy.

A grandfather clock in the manor had three hands instead of two. The third hand moved very slowly and reached the top of the dial only once a generation. The household measured certain events by the third hand. Births, marriages, deaths. Everything else was measured by the other two.

There had been a Tuesday, but the Tuesday had been quietly removed. The calendar showed Monday and then Wednesday. No one remembered what had happened on the missing day. Some claimed they had a small unfinished feeling, a hunger or a thought that had not arrived. They attributed it to the Tuesday.

A church bell that rang at the wrong hour, but consistently. The villagers had set their day by the wrong-hour bell for so long that the village's noon was now the rest of the world's three. The villagers were not late. The world was off by three hours, in their reckoning.

The clock on the mantle ran backward, slowly. No one had reset it. The household had agreed that the backward time was its own kind of correct. Births in this house were celebrated at the backward birthday. The dead were remembered at the backward death-day, before they had arrived.

The calendar in the schoolhouse had a thirty-second day every spring. The children were given a holiday on it. They did not know what they did on the thirty-second; they remembered only the return. The teachers said the day was for being elsewhere. The teachers did not specify where.

A wristwatch worn by a man who had stopped winding it years ago. The watch told the time anyway. He claimed the watch had memorized the rhythm of his life and now produced time from inside itself. Doctors who examined the watch found nothing. The watch continued.

There was a year that had not happened. It was between two other years. Records of it existed — letters dated, receipts written — but nothing had occurred. The towns went on as if. The year that had not happened was retained on the calendars out of politeness.

The egg timer in the kitchen ran longer than three minutes when the cook needed it to. The cook had not asked the timer to do this. The timer simply understood. The eggs were always right.

A clock in the train station ran fast for arrivals and slow for departures. The travelers did not know this consciously, but they were never late and never early. The clock served the trains, not the time.

Sundials in the garden whose shadows fell at the wrong angle. The sun was overhead. The shadows pointed sideways, eastward, away from the sun. The gardener had stopped trying to correct them. The sundials had their own opinion about where the sun was.

There was an hourglass on a desk that had not been turned in three decades, but whose sand still fell. The sand fell upward sometimes. The desk's owner had moved away long ago. The hourglass remained.

The calendar in the corridor had Tuesdays printed twice. A first Tuesday and a second Tuesday. Most weeks the residents experienced only one of them. The other Tuesday, they said, was for the cat.

A clock that struck thirteen at midnight. The thirteenth strike was always a half-strike, hesitant, and the household knew to be quiet through it. After the thirteenth, the night belonged to the house, and the house used the night for its own purposes.

The watchmaker's shop displayed a clock that ran in only one direction depending on who was looking at it. Customers said the time. The watchmaker did not contradict them. He sold the same clock to several customers, and each took it home reading differently.

A stopwatch left running on a table. No one had pressed start. The watch claimed that the moment of starting had occurred long before anyone arrived. The watch was patient and did not require justification.

Inside the music box, time had stopped at the moment the lid closed. When the lid opened, time resumed. The music continued from where it had left off. The dancers, if there had been dancers, returned to their positions.

The kitchen clock and the bedroom clock disagreed by twelve minutes. They had disagreed for years. The household had not adjusted either. The twelve minutes were the household's, kept between the two rooms, occupied by no one.
