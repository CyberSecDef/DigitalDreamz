The room had one window. The window faced a garden. The garden faced another window. The other window faced the room. This was the room's only feature. Everything in the room — the table, the chair, the cup on the table — was incidental to the looking-back-at-itself that defined the room.

There was a house with seven rooms. Whoever entered the seventh found themselves in the first. Whoever entered the first sometimes found themselves in the seventh. The house was not infinite. The house was simply not directional. Visitors had learned, over the years, to stop expecting it to be.

In the corner of the kitchen, where two walls met, a third wall had begun. No one remembered it being built. The third wall was the same color as the others and as old as the others, but the room was now triangular where it had been rectangular, and the corner now had no corner. The kettle had moved itself to make space. The kitchen accepted the change without comment.

The library had a reading room where the books read each other. Visitors entering would notice that the books on the shelves were not where they had been left, that pages turned without hands, that occasionally a marginal note appeared in a book that no one had opened. The librarian, when asked about this, said only that the books were in conversation, and that interrupting them would be discourteous.

Behind the wallpaper of the upstairs bedroom, the wall continued, but it was a different wall. Whoever had pasted the wallpaper had not been in the same room as the wall they had pasted it onto. The wallpaper bridged the rooms. The pattern repeated in both, but slightly out of register, as though one wall were dreaming the other.

In the hallway, a clock that ran on the heartbeats of whoever walked past. When the hallway was empty, the clock stopped. When two walked past at once, the clock ran fast, and the hallway felt longer than it was. When no one had walked past for a long time, the clock had to be reminded what time it was, and would sometimes settle on a time that did not correspond to any clock elsewhere.

A staircase descended from the attic to the cellar without passing through any of the floors between. Whoever began at the top arrived at the bottom without having visited the rooms in between. The rooms in between continued to exist. They were simply not on the staircase.

There was a bedroom in the house that no one slept in. The bedroom continued to be furnished. The bed was made. The lamp on the bedside table was sometimes on. Whoever maintained the room did so without being seen, and whatever the bed remembered, the bed kept to itself.

The dining room was longer than the house. This was difficult to explain to surveyors but the dining room had been longer than the house for as long as the house had been standing. The table at one end and the table at the other were both the same table. Whoever sat at the far end was not far. Whoever sat at the near end was not near.

A small room at the back of the house, never used, accumulated weather. Storms from years past lingered there. A rain from a winter long over still fell, gentle, in one corner. A summer afternoon's warmth still hung in the air near the window. The room was the house's archive of weathers, and no one cleaned it, because cleaning would have meant choosing which weather to keep.

The threshold of the front door wept slightly when crossed by anyone who would not return. The threshold did not weep when crossed by visitors who would come back. The household had learned to read the threshold. Departures of consequence were marked by it, before they were known to be of consequence.

In one of the rooms, the floor was a sky. Whoever entered did not fall. The sky held them as a floor would. Looking down, far below, were the tops of clouds, and beyond the clouds, the upturned ceiling of an unrelated house, and beyond that, more sky. The room had been a floor for some time, and would be a sky for some time more, and after that no one knew what the room would decide.

The pantry contained nothing that had been put there. Whatever was needed appeared. Whatever was not needed did not. The household ate well. The pantry asked nothing. The pantry's only requirement was that no one open it expecting a particular thing. Particular expectations made the pantry empty.

A room that had been a different room before any of the household could remember. The room remembered. Sometimes, in certain lights, the previous room showed through — a different wallpaper, a different window, a different chair. The household had learned not to comment. The current room was the room they lived in. The previous room was patient.
