The cat had been a teacup. Before that, a window. Before that, the cat. Each form was complete in itself. The cat did not regret the teacup, nor did the teacup regret the window. Continuity was not a virtue here. The cat purred where the teacup had cooled.

A field of grass that, on closer examination, turned out to be a sea. The sea, on closer examination, was a field of grass. Neither examination was wrong. The walker through the field-sea simply walked, and the walking shifted the substance under the foot from blade to wave and back, and the walker did not stumble.

The book had been a tree. This was unremarkable; many books had been trees. What was unusual was that the book remembered being the tree, and the pages occasionally rustled in a way the binding could not account for. The reader noticed. The reader continued reading. The book continued remembering.

Between the breath in and the breath out, there was a moment when the body was not anyone's. The body waited. The body considered the possibility of being something else — a window perhaps, or a long quiet road, or the silence after a bell. Then the breath came back, and the body was the body again, and whatever the body had considered was forgotten or pretended forgotten.

The river had been a road. The villagers remembered walking it. Now they crossed it in boats, and when they walked the road that had replaced the river, they walked carefully, as though afraid the road would forget itself and become a river again, mid-stride.

A name pronounced backward became another name. The other name belonged to a person who had not yet been born. When the person was born, the name was given to them, and the original name was forgotten by everyone except a single dictionary in a library no one used.

The mountain in the distance had been a cloud. Eventually the cloud would be a mountain again. The villagers below built no permanent structures. They had learned to read the long weathers, and the long weathers had taught them that permanence was a kind of forgetting.

A child grew into a tree. This was rare but not unheard of. The tree remembered being the child. The child's parents visited the tree on weekends, brought it small offerings, sat beneath it, spoke to it as they had spoken to the child. After many years, the tree began growing leaves shaped like small hands.

The sentence had been a shape. Before that, a sound. Before that, an absence the speaker had needed to fill. After being a sentence, it would be a memory, and after that, the suggestion of a memory, and after that, a slight discoloration in the air of the room where it had been said.

A crow that occasionally was a bell. When the crow flew, it was a crow. When the crow rested on the eaves of the church, it was a bell. The villagers had grown accustomed. They tolled the crow at noon. The crow flew at midnight, calling the dawn that was still six hours away.

The chair on which the visitor sat had once been the visitor. They were not aware of this, and the chair did not say. The chair held them politely, supported them as a chair should, and made no sign. After the visit, the chair returned to being a chair only, the visitor having taken back whatever had been chair-shaped in them.

A loaf of bread that, halfway through being eaten, became the memory of a loaf of bread. The eater finished eating. The eater was no longer hungry. The bread was complete in having been eaten and complete in being remembered. Both were forms of bread.

The horse in the field was sometimes a horse and sometimes a stone shaped like a horse. The farmer did not insist. The farmer noted whether the horse was horse-shaped or stone-shaped that day and proceeded accordingly. On stone days the farmer worked the field alone. On horse days the farmer rode.

A song heard in childhood that had no melody when remembered. The melody had been a melody at the time. The remembering had unmade it. The words remained. The words were now the song, and the song was something else, and what the song had been was now elsewhere, perhaps in another childhood.

Each morning the cup on the table was a slightly different cup. The household did not notice, because each morning the household was a slightly different household, and the cup matched. Continuity was negotiated daily. Most days the negotiation succeeded. The cup was always there. The cup was never the same.
