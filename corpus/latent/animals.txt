A cat that had been in the house longer than any of the residents. No one had brought it. It had been there when the first of them arrived. It was old, but did not appear to be aging. The cat was not consulted on household matters, but the household was aware it had opinions.

A bird outside the kitchen window that sang the same six notes every morning. The notes were not in any songbook. The bird had been singing them for at least a generation. The household had begun, without intending to, to hum the notes themselves, in the bird's absence.

There was a fox that crossed the garden each evening at dusk. No one had ever seen it arrive or leave. It simply walked, from one fence to the other, without urgency. The household referred to its passage as the day's punctuation.

A dog that slept in the front hall, although no one in the household owned a dog. The dog was always there in the morning. No one asked where it came from. It did not eat, did not drink, did not mark its presence in any way except by being there.

Bees in the wall of the parlor, audible but invisible. The bees had been there for so long that the household no longer noticed the humming. Visitors heard it and asked. The household had to listen, then, to hear it again.

A horse standing in the field behind the house. No one had ever seen it move. The grass was not eaten where it stood. It had stood there for a year, perhaps longer. The household had stopped wondering.

Mice that did not eat the household's food but rearranged it. A loaf of bread might be turned by morning. A jar of preserves moved to a different shelf. The household had ceased to find this distressing. The arrangement was usually an improvement.

The owl in the eaves was wooden. It had been carved by someone in the family, long ago. It sometimes turned its head to follow whoever walked across the yard. Carvings did not normally do this. The owl was an exception.

A school of small silver fish in the well. They had no apparent source of food. The well was deep enough that they must have been fed by something. The household drew water from the well and the fish were undisturbed. The water was sweet.

There was a moth in the library that had been alive for at least three years. The librarian had kept track. Moths normally lived a few weeks. This one returned each evening to the same shelf, the same book, the same page. The page was not turned. The moth read patiently.

A black cat that crossed the room at the same hour every afternoon, regardless of whether the door was open. The cat did not pause. The cat did not look at the household. The cat was passing through, on its way somewhere only the cat knew.

The frogs in the pond sang in unison, not the usual chorus. They began at once and ended at once. The household had never determined the cue. Some said the frogs had a leader; some said they listened to something else, something not in the pond.

A spider in the corner of the kitchen that no one disturbed. The web was repaired each morning. The spider had been there longer than any of the residents. It was considered, by household consensus, a small but present member.

Pigeons on the roof that arrived only on certain days. The household tracked the days. There was no pattern any of them could identify, but the pigeons knew. On the days the pigeons came, certain things were possible that were not possible otherwise.

The dog at the neighbor's house barked only at one corner of the fence, where there was nothing. The dog had been doing this for a year. The corner was unremarkable. The household, when asked, said the dog probably saw what dogs see. They did not specify what that was.

A salamander in the well. Whoever lowered the bucket sometimes brought it up. The salamander would be cool, calm, slightly damp. It did not struggle. It would be returned to the water. The next morning, again sometimes, it would come up.

There was a deer that came to the edge of the yard at dawn and stood for an hour. The household watched it from the kitchen window. The deer watched the household. Neither approached. After an hour the deer would turn and walk away, and the household would begin breakfast.

A rabbit that lived under the porch, and was sometimes white and sometimes brown. No one ever saw it change. The household did not know if it was the same rabbit.

The crows in the field had a particular tree they preferred. They gathered at dusk, on certain days, in numbers that exceeded the local population of crows. The household assumed they came from elsewhere. The crows did not say.

A small lizard on the wall by the window that turned its head when spoken to, although it did not respond. The lizard had been there for the better part of a year. It listened. The household did not know whether the listening helped or hindered the lizard.
