The kettle had memorized the morning. It boiled itself when someone entered the kitchen at the right hour, even when no one had set it. The household had stopped asking how. The tea was always ready.

There was a chair in the parlor that no one was permitted to sit in. The household called it the visiting chair. It was for the visitor who had not yet arrived. Some said the chair had been waiting for so long that the visitor was now made of waiting, and would arrive only when the waiting ended.

A pen on the desk that wrote in a hand that was not its owner's. Letters drafted with the pen came out in a stranger's penmanship. The owner had stopped using it for personal correspondence. The pen was used now only for letters one did not want to be recognized as one's own.

The umbrella in the stand had not been opened in years. When it finally was opened, indoors, by accident, rain fell from it for several minutes. The rain wet only the carpet beneath the umbrella. The carpet did not grow mold.

A pair of shoes by the door that walked themselves to a different position each night. By morning they were always back at the door, but slightly turned. The household followed the angle of their turning as a kind of compass. When the shoes pointed inward, no one went out that day.

The piano in the music room played one note when no one was in the room. The note was always the same. No one had ever caught the piano playing. Mid-conversation in the next room, the note would sound, and the conversation would pause, and then continue.

A thimble that fit any finger. Children, adults, the elderly. The thimble adjusted. It had been in the household for generations, and was the most-borrowed object in the sewing box. No one had thought to ask whose finger it had originally fit.

The kitchen scale weighed objects accurately, but also weighed their absence. A bowl removed from the scale registered a negative reading equal to the bowl's mass. The cook had learned to use this. Recipes called for a deficit of flour, a withdrawal of salt.

A lamp that turned itself off when read to. The reading was the only signal. Speech, music, conversation did not affect it. Someone reading aloud near it caused it to dim to extinction. The household read silently in its presence.

The grandfather clock had a small drawer at its base. The drawer was not a working drawer; it could not be opened. Inside the drawer, the household believed, was the time the clock had not yet told. When the clock stopped, the drawer would open, and what was inside would be released.

A teaspoon that bent slightly when held by someone tired. The bend was unmistakable, although temporary. The teaspoon straightened in the hand of someone rested. The household used it as a diagnostic. Children were given the teaspoon to hold before bedtime to test their fatigue.

The painting in the dining room had a small bird in the upper right corner. The bird had been there as long as the painting. The bird had also been moving very slowly across the canvas for as long as the painting had hung. Currently it was halfway. By the time the bird reached the left edge, the household would be different.

A coat hung on a hook by the door, never worn, that aged. The cloth thinned. The buttons tarnished. The hem frayed. The owner had moved away decades ago. The coat continued to age in his absence, faithfully, as if he were still wearing it elsewhere.

The clock on the mantle ticked at two different rhythms — one for daylight, one for night. Daylight was faster. The household's days were short and its nights long, by the clock's measure. No one minded. The arrangement suited.

A music box that played a different tune each time it was wound. The tunes were unfamiliar. None had been recognized by any visitor. The music box knew songs that no one alive knew, and was patient about teaching them.

The mirror in the hallway lost its silvering one square inch at a time. Whoever stood before it saw a small dark patch, and the patch grew slightly between visits. Eventually the mirror would be entirely black. The household had not yet decided what to do then. Possibly nothing.

A pair of scissors on the kitchen counter that cut only what it wanted to cut. Paper, sometimes. String, occasionally. Cloth, never. The cook had learned not to attempt cloth. The scissors were for the kitchen's own purposes, which it had not disclosed.

There was a vase on the windowsill that held flowers no one had cut. Each morning the vase contained flowers the household did not recognize, fresh, sometimes still wet. By evening the flowers would have wilted. By morning, new ones.

A book on the shelf whose cover changed color depending on the room's temperature. In a cool room, deep blue. In a warm room, faded green. The book had not been read in some time. The household consulted it, but only by looking at it.

The door knocker on the front door knocked itself sometimes, late at night. The household no longer answered when this happened. The knocking was for itself, they had learned, and answering it was discourteous.
