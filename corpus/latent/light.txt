A lamp in the corner that had not been lit but illuminated anyway. The light was faint, the color of sun through old paper. The lamp had not been plugged in for a long time. The corner had decided to be lit, with or without the lamp's permission.

The candle burned without diminishing. It had been burning for the length of three lifetimes, the household claimed. The wax neither melted nor reformed. The wick neither shortened nor regrew. The candle was simply lit, in the same way some things are simply blue.

A window that admitted a different light than the light outside. The garden beyond the window was sunlit, but the room behind the window was lit as if by candle. The window mediated. The window had its own preference.

There was a corridor in the house that grew darker the further one walked into it, even when it was the middle of the day. Visitors carried lamps. The lamps dimmed as the corridor deepened. At the end, the lamp went out, but the visitor could still see — by what light, none could say.

The moon rising once over the rooftops was the wrong moon. It was crescent when the moon outside was full. No one in the house remarked on it. Different houses had different moons. The moons had distributed themselves long ago.

A lantern carried through the orchard cast no shadow of the carrier. The branches threw their shadows. The fruit threw its shadows. The lantern-bearer alone walked unshadowed. They were not alarmed. They had been carrying the lantern for years.

The light from the kitchen at midnight was the wrong color. It was blue when it should have been yellow, the residents said, and no bulb in the house produced blue light. The kitchen lit itself, on certain nights, in colors the kitchen preferred.

There had been a small star above the village, lower than the others. It had been there for as long as anyone could remember. Astronomers from the city, when consulted, said the star was not catalogued and could not exist. The village continued to navigate by it.

A reading lamp that brightened slightly when an interesting passage was being read. Readers had grown reliant on it as a critic. Books that did not interest the lamp were set aside, regardless of the reader's judgment.

The chandelier in the ballroom lit only when there was no one in the ballroom. Whoever entered to investigate found it dark. The dance, the residents said, was for the chandelier itself. The chandelier had its own guests, who came when the room was empty.

A flashlight that worked only when pointed at the ceiling. Pointed at the floor, it dimmed. Pointed at a person, it went out entirely. The flashlight had its own opinions about what should be illuminated.

The streetlamp on the corner had been broken for thirty years, but still glowed faintly on certain nights. The maintenance crew, when called, reported finding the bulb broken and the wires cut. The faint glow continued. They had stopped responding to calls about that lamp.

Inside the well, an unexpected light. Whoever leaned over the edge saw, far below, what looked like the inside of a lit room. Furniture, a small table, a chair. Nothing moved in the room. The room was not reflected. It was simply at the bottom of the well.

The sun rose twice on the morning of the festival. First at its usual time, then again two hours later, brighter than the first. The villagers remarked on it but did not investigate. The festival continued through both sunrises.

There was a corner of the garden that was always in shadow, regardless of the sun's position. The plants there grew differently. The gardener tended them with an extra patience, knowing the corner had decided long ago to be elsewhere.

A jar of fireflies kept on the windowsill, glowing in the day and dim at night. The fireflies had reversed themselves, for reasons of their own. The household used the jar as a clock of sorts, although a wrong one.

The hallway light came on a moment before anyone reached the switch. By the time the hand touched the switch, the light was already on. No one had ever touched the switch in time to turn it on themselves. The hallway anticipated.

A painting of a candle in a dark room. In low light, the painted candle gave off enough light to read the title of the painting. In bright light, the painted candle was just a painting. The gallery curator, when asked, said the candle was a private matter.

The lighthouse on the cliff had not had a keeper in twenty years, and had no electric lamp, and yet the light circled every evening. Sailors used it. Officials denied it. The lighthouse continued, unconcerned with both.

A child's nightlight, plugged into the wall, that lit a room not its own. The child's room was dim. Somewhere, in another house, in another country perhaps, a different room was being lit. The child slept anyway.
